Introduction

Mount Rainier is an active stratovolcano located in the Cascade Range of Washington State. It stands at roughly 14,410 feet (4,392m) and has been shaped by tectonic, volcanic, glacial, and erosion processes. The magma that fuels the eruptions along the Cascade Volcanic Arc is generated by the Juan de Fuca Plate subducting beneath the North American Plate. The past several hundred thousand years have seen repeated eruptions of lava for Rainier, the slopes have been sculpted by 25+ named glaciers that constantly modify the landscape. This website examines the tectonic setting, volcanic formation, glaciation, erosion, hazards, and geological map evidence to explain how the processes interact to produce modern-day Rainier.

Tectonic Setting

The volcano lies within the Cascade Volcanic Arc, a chain of volcanoes formed by the Juan de Fuca plate subducting beneath the North American Plate at a convergent boundary, the Cascadia Subduction Zone. The magma at Rainier is classified as intermediate, with a silica content between 52% and 63%. This means that the lava flows are moderately viscous with explosive eruptions. Rainier is the second most seismically active in the Cascade Range, experiencing volcanic tremors caused by the movement of magma beneath the summit, or tectonic earthquakes, often occurring in the West Rainier Seismic Zone.

Google Earth elevation and distance measurements show that Mount Rainier rises more than 12,000 feet above surrounding lowland valleys over a relatively short horizontal distance. This steep relief supports its interpretation as a stratovolcano constructed by repeated eruptions. Additionally, U-shaped valleys and a radial drainage pattern visible in oblique Google Earth imagery indicate that extensive glacial erosion has modified the original volcanic cone.

Volcanic Formation

Mount Rainier is a stratovolcano (composite volcano), built over the last 500,000 years through a series of individual eruptions. The ancient base is formed by much older rocks, including the Tatoosh Granodiorite (14-18 million years old) and volcanic formations from over 36 million years ago. The mountain was formed in four major stages, each lasting roughly 100,000 years. Initially, there was a period of rapid lava accumulation that quickly built the volcano to a similar height that it stands at today. Then, another high-output phase called Mowich, where the volcano may have reached its greatest height, theorized as 2,000 feet taller than the current summit. Following those stages, activity slowed and instead features like the Little Tahoma Peak were formed. Glaciation & Erosion

The current glacial status of Mount Rainier shows that the mountain’s summit has likely declined by over 20 feet since the mid-20th century due to melting ice and snow. Historically, the movement of the glaciers has carved deep and wide U-shaped valleys out of what were originally V-shaped river canyons. The rugged appearance comes from glacial scouring creating steep-walled basins known as cirques. The thick glaciers filled the valleys and forced lava to flow along their margins; when the ice melted, these same lava flows were left as high-standing ridges, resulting in a phenomenon known as inverted topography. The glacial melting and subsequent erosion involved a large amount of sediment transport downstream. This leads to aggradation, riverbeds in surrounding lowlands rise as they fill with gravel and sand. This increases the long-term risk of flooding in the Puget Sound region. Lahars

Due to its potential for lahars, Mount Rainier is widely regarded as one of the most dangerous volcanoes in the world. These flows are a greater risk to the Puget Sound region than actual lava or ash. Lahars, in this context, can travel at speeds over 20 mph, reaching depths of up to 400 feet. The Osceola Mudflow, ~5600 years ago, was 460 feet deep, reaching as far as present-day Auburn and the Puget Sound.
